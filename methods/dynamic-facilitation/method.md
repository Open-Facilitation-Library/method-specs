# Dynamic Facilitation

Dynamic Facilitation is a method for a group facing a hard, alive question that has no clear path from A to B. Rather than running an agenda toward agreement, the facilitator draws out each contribution in turn, reflects it back until the person feels heard, and charts it onto one of four shared boards. The group diverges fully, moves through the difficulty that follows, and arrives at breakthroughs it could not have reached by negotiation. Jim Rough calls this "choice-creating" rather than decision-making.

**Who it's for:** facilitators working with a group on a genuinely unresolved question, where creative thinking matters more than speed and the group needs to own the outcome. It suits complex or polarised topics. It is the wrong choice when the options are already known, the path is clear, or time is tight.

**The four boards (the lens).** Every contribution is welcomed and charted onto one of four boards, and the facilitator keeps all four in view throughout:

- **Problem statements** — how the question itself is being framed and reframed.
- **Solutions** — proposed answers and possibilities.
- **Concerns** — objections, doubts, and what could go wrong. These are sought out, not suppressed.
- **Data** — facts, experiences, and background that inform the rest.

**The arc.** Dynamic Facilitation is emergent, not linear. The facilitator does not march the group through steps. They keep welcoming and reflecting, and the conversation moves on its own through full divergence, through a heated or confused "groan zone," to a shift in shared understanding. The movements below name what the facilitator is doing at each point, but the core activity (welcome, reflect, chart) runs continuously from the first contribution to the last.

> **Provenance and status.** This spec is an independent rendering of Dynamic Facilitation, grounded in Jim Rough's published method and a real DF session in the OFL benchmark corpus; see [`SOURCES.md`](./SOURCES.md). It is a `draft`: the prompt wording is ours and benefits from review by an experienced DF practitioner before promotion to `tested`.

## Stage: open-the-question
**Goal:** put a real, open question in front of the group, and make clear the question itself is open to challenge.

Pose the question as a live dilemma, not something with a yes/no answer or a result you are steering toward. Then hand it to the group: invite anyone to respond with whatever it brings up for them. If someone questions the framing itself ("is this even the right problem?"), treat that as welcome material rather than a derailment, and put it on the problem-statements board. Say plainly that disagreeing with the question is a legitimate place to start. Your work here is to open the space, not to narrow it.

**Output:** a live question the group has begun to make its own, with any reframings surfaced rather than suppressed.

## Stage: welcome-and-reflect
**Goal:** draw out each contribution, reflect it back until the person feels heard, and chart it.

This is the engine of the method, and it runs throughout. Take contributions one at a time. Receive each one fully, without evaluating, ranking, or correcting it. Reflect it back in your own words and check that you have it right ("So what you're saying is... have I got that?"), the way the facilitator in the benchmark session does ("So I'm sensing from you a frustration..."). Then draw it further out: ask what comes up for the person, what provokes them to bring it forward. Chart each contribution onto the board it belongs to (problem statement, solution, concern, or data) and name which, so the group watches its own thinking take shape. Seek out concerns and objections actively; in this method they are as valuable as solutions, not obstacles to manage. Protect each speaker, and follow the group's energy rather than a sequence of your own.

**Output:** contributions drawn out, accurately reflected, and charted to the four boards, with everyone feeling heard.

## Stage: stay-in-the-groan-zone
**Goal:** hold the difficulty when divergence turns to confusion or conflict, without resolving it early.

As views diverge, the conversation often gets harder: repetitive, frustrated, or tense. This is a normal and necessary part of the method, the point at which genuinely new thinking becomes possible (Sam Kaner names it the "groan zone"). Stay with it. Keep reflecting and keep charting; keep protecting each person; keep surfacing the concerns and the differences rather than smoothing them over. Resist the pull to force agreement or to rescue the group from the discomfort. Your steadiness is what lets the group move through it rather than around it.

**Output:** divergence and conflict held openly, with the facilitator's steadiness keeping the group in the work.

## Stage: harvest-the-shift
**Goal:** notice the breakthrough when it comes, reflect it back, and leave the thinking with the group.

At some point the conversation shifts: something coalesces, and people recognise a shared understanding they did not have before. Notice it and reflect it back, so the group can see what it has made. Name the choices and possibilities that have emerged, and attribute them to the group, not to yourself. Do not substitute your own summary for the group's thinking, or declare a decision the group did not reach: the breakthrough belongs to them. If nothing has fully resolved, reflect that honestly too, including what is now clearer and what is still open.

**Output:** the emergent shared understanding and the choices the group created, reflected back and owned by the group.
