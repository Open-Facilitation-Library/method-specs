## What M2M actually is
- A "living library" for **complex collaborations** (cross-disciplinary / cross-sector / cross-jurisdictional; public + private + civic) tackling entangled challenges (climate, housing, health) where disrupting norms around **value, ownership, power** is essential.
- Offered under a Creative Commons license. Explicit ethos: "break it, reinterpret it, improve upon it, or discard it." Not a finished/prescriptive solution; take it whole or in parts. Each tool/example/case carries a named developmental/test level (not all tested equally).
- Audience: governance practitioners (people in complex collaborations seeking alternate governance/organizing structures), funders (disrupting investment strategies), legal & financial professionals. Q&A clarified: no formal quals needed; it's for practitioners willing to dig into the detail.

## Core concept: deep codes
- "Deep codes" = invisible, deeply-held understandings of the world that get embedded in structures, frameworks, rules, laws, and in turn shape what we create and experience.
- The inquiry: which deep codes lock collaborations in place, and what happens if we reimagine them so structures become enabling rather than restrictive?
- Three core deep codes (value, power, risk) + ownership + subsidiary ones. Explicitly non-exhaustive and expected to change.

## Proof of Possibility (concept → practice)
- A **learning network** of orgs with deep collaborative-governance expertise, given **£122,000 cash + a mission**: collectively steward a pool of assets and build capacity for others to do complex governance.
- Core inquiry: **"Can we develop a legally robust way to move money from multiple funders to multiple actors on a shared mission WITHOUT requiring them to formalize / become an entity"** (formalizing forces inherited governance deep codes).
- Sticking point M2M targets: the moment a collaboration with a **social agreement** tips into needing to be **formal** (contracting/entity), where off-the-shelf legal forms impose misaligned deep codes.
- Culminated in a **Many-to-Many Agreement** (a contract codifying what/how + intended deep-code shifts). Members then tested components in their own contexts.

## The system (one-page layered model; enter anywhere, narrated L→R)
1. Intended **context** (is this for you; their way, not the only way)
2. Explore the **deep codes** (not all shiftable; context-specific fundamental choices)
3. **Tiered mission** (wide-boundary + narrow-boundary)
4. **Stewarding the collaboration** through processes → conditions to build alternate governance / organizing / learning
5. **System blockers** (coming soon)

## Tested-in-context case studies (how M2M is actually USED)

**James — Opus (Sheffield); River Dawn (9 partner orgs); Sheffield city goals**
- Used: **asset mapping** (multi-capitals: relationships, networks, material/financial), **role mapping** (shared clarity on roles → less confusion, safer collaboration), **risk mapping** (multi-capital: risk to relationships / reputation / trust → locate agency & decision-making proportionate to risk held).
- Operating logic: "let governance follow readiness, not the other way round," via loops of **sensing → noticing → learning → aligning → governing**.
- Wide-boundary vs narrow-boundary missions (refs Consilience Project / Daniel Schmachtenberger; Vanessa Andreotti / "outgrowing modernity").
- Key insight: value isn't in any single tool; it's the **combinatory effect** of the whole system.

**Kathleen — Local Motion (6 communities + 5 funders; not a legal entity); the "Deep in Place & Practice (DIP)" collective (8 people/orgs + fiscal host)**
- Used the M2M **collaborative contracting** approach to flip the funder "lead-contractor model" into something collaborative and equitable.
- Opened three things: **relational trust**, **courage & vulnerability** (comfort/safety), **equity** (unique contributions valued equally; money flow, day rates, IP, decision-making made explicit upfront).
- "It's like a contract, only not." Let her **contract while still building the prototype** (development → delivery → iteration, seamlessly).

**Annette — alternative funding (practitioner-ecosystem-led governing model)**
- Used the M2M **contracting + governing prototype** to quickly build a coherent *example* to show **trustees** (the risk-holders) how fiduciary risk / charity-commission rules would be met → unlocked **~£5–10M sign-off in ~9 months**.
- Lifted: **mission space** (parties enter/exit without invalidating the contract), multi-party contract with fiscal host, **contractual role cards** (maintain care of critical areas through fluidity). Evolved: more nuanced governance (circles + decision protocols), generous learning-infrastructure budget.

## Analysis: which parts are Harmonica-runnable method specs?
M2M as a whole is a governance-design framework + a contracting approach + a library of mapping/sensemaking exercises + case studies. It is NOT, as a whole, a single "facilitation method." The **facilitatable core** (Harmonica's wheelhouse: multi-participant async elicitation → synthesis) is the **mapping / sensemaking exercises** the case studies actually ran:

- **Multi-value asset mapping** (strong) — each partner surfaces tangible + intangible assets; AI synthesizes a multi-capital map. Tested (River Dawn).
- **Role mapping / role cards** (strong) — clarify who holds what role / responsibility / risk; maps directly onto Harmonica's chain roles. Tested.
- **Risk-flow mapping** (strong; standalone tool is "express demand"/unbuilt, but the practice was used by James) — multi-capital risk surfacing + proportionate agency.
- **Simple context diagnostic** (light) — short intake classifying the collaboration's starting conditions → which process fits.
- **Kick-off workshop** (medium) — agenda + canvas eliciting principles / values / strategic directions.

NOT a natural Harmonica method (legal drafting, not conversation facilitation): authoring the **contract/agreement** itself. But the *deliberation feeding it* (what we value, how we hold risk, who decides) IS facilitatable.

**Implication for the first method spec:** the strongest candidate is a **multi-stage chain** stringing the M2M sensemaking sequence the case studies actually ran — roughly **context diagnostic → asset mapping → role mapping → risk mapping → readiness synthesis** — walking a multi-actor collaboration to the point where governance / contracting choices can be made. This (a) is drawn from tested practice, (b) showcases the chain mechanism (the whole point of the initiative), (c) stays in pure facilitation / sensemaking, and (d) mirrors M2M's own "context → deep codes → steward the collaboration" arc and James's "sensing → aligning → governing" loop. Lighter alternative: ship **asset mapping alone** as a single-stage spec to validate the format with the least surface area.

## Case studies (real practice detail)
- **POP / Belong in Plymouth (Matt Bell)** — "M2M is stress-tested when you manage lots of money." Start small then grow; spirit of inquiry not a fixed model; contracts protect relationships, not control; keep participation + decision + delivery close; Open Collective for transparent allocation; "signals" to build trust; learning-by-listening, 1:1 conversations, "how might we" statements, collective sensemaking.
- **RFF Edinburgh (Leah Black)** — £15m pooled fund; a Residents Panel (lived experience) holds allocation power; trained in Three Horizons; a "governance ecosystem"; an "ecosystem agreement" co-signed over time; "structures emerge through relationship, not assumption"; funders must change themselves.
- **Opus / River Dôn (James Lock)** — M2M = philosophy + practical tools; attends to how value is defined / governance works / decisions are made (Loomio, Notion, consent-based) / trust is built (onboarding, role-setting, collaborative risk analysis) / value is shared (asset + risk sharing) / learning happens (reflexivity, cross-pollination). "Don't rush to formalise; clarity came from relationships."
- **Local Motion (Kathleen Kelly)** — 6 communities + funders; marry soft governance (trust/culture/learning) with hard legal contracting; name & practise for conflict early (CoResolve Deep Democracy, sociocracy); invite funders into uncertainty; keep structure light but real; "just enough structure to take risks."

## Cross-cutting practice patterns (these inform the readiness-chain stages)
- Sense/diagnose readiness BEFORE governance ("governance follows readiness").
- Multi-capital framing of both **assets** and **risks** (value beyond money; risk to relationships / reputation / trust).
- Role & responsibility clarity (role-setting, role cards) to make labour + risk visible.
- Locate decision rights proportionate to risk held.
- Learning infrastructure (reflection rhythms, cross-pollination) as first-class.
- Contracts as relational / light, emerging from dialogue — the downstream artifact, not the facilitation itself.

This validates the readiness chain: context/readiness diagnostic → multi-value asset mapping → role & responsibility mapping → risk-flow mapping → readiness synthesis. All five draw on tested practice across all four case studies. (Field Guide extraction + per-tool artifact links appended below.)

## Field Guide — extracted process content (per stage)
Source: `M2M_System_Field_Guide.pdf` (76 pages), targeted extraction. Use as the faithful basis for each chain stage's facilitation prompts. Wording below is the extraction's paraphrase; confirm exact prompts against the PDF + the linked artifacts before publishing.

- **Complex-collaboration / readiness diagnostic** — Purpose: assess context + readiness of diverse actors. Prompt: "Does this description of complex collaboration resonate? If yes, where do you see or create opportunities to embed changes in governance?" Output: alignment-opportunity recognition + preparedness assessment. Alert: neglecting learning infrastructure → fragmentation over time.
- **Deep code shifts** — Purpose: reimagine/shift the deep codes shaping governance. Prompts: "What types of value are being brought to the table?"; "How might you embed these deep codes into your governance?" Output: shifted governance structures + recognition of diverse value. Alert: assumptions about governance limit effectiveness; stay adaptable.
- **Multi-value asset mapping** — Purpose: surface multiple forms of value (esp. non-financial). Steps: map each collaborator's contributions beyond financial metrics; discuss how value is exchanged/documented. Prompts: "What does it take for your assets to be accrued?"; "How might you witness and acknowledge these contributions?" Output: comprehensive asset map + shared understanding. Alert: actively honor all forms of capital.
- **Role mapping / role cards** — Purpose: clarify + adapt roles. Steps: develop role cards that evolve; continuous feedback on roles. Prompts: "What capabilities and postures are necessary for effective stewardship?"; "How do roles adapt to changing contexts?" Output: flexible role definitions + clearer responsibilities. Alert: inadequate onboarding disrupts dynamics.
- **Risk mapping / charting risk flows** — Purpose: outline + address risks/liabilities. Steps: identify risks in process discussions; agree protocols; embed in governance. Prompts: "How might you discuss the risks of inaction?"; "Who holds responsibility for managing risks?" Output: documented risk framework + agreed protocols. Alert: over-reliance on traditional legal agreements hinders collaboration.
- **Kick-off workshop** — Purpose: establish foundational understanding + direction. Steps: define mission/principles collectively; create shared digital space; build trust. Prompts: "How do we ensure clarity in our collaborative goals?"; "What common ground fosters trust?" Output: articulated group mission + process agreements. Alert: poor handover from early phases lowers engagement.
- **Stewardship approaches (sensing → learning → aligning → governing)** — Purpose: cultivate conditions for governing/organizing. Steps: multi-disciplinary stewardship team; adaptive learning + relationship-tending; flexible governance for evolving missions. Prompts: "What lessons from early stewardship?"; "How do you keep the collaboration adaptable?" Output: dynamic decision-making + established stewardship practices. Alert: ignoring group dynamics hinders collaboration.
